  Why performance dropped on 20×20

  Your "fewer meaningful interactions" intuition is correct, but it's one of ~4 things, and not the easiest to fix:

  1. Your horizon is hardcoded for 10×10. step_limit = 100 and the speed bonus max(0.0, (50 - steps)/50). On 20×20, BFS path lengths roughly double, so the key→guard-detour→exit route can eat most of 100 steps — and steps > 50 almost always, so the speed bonus is permanently 0. You silently deleted a
  chunk of the win incentive. This alone can tank learning.
  2. Interaction density drops 4×. Same 2 melee + 1 guard + 1 warlock over 4× the area → the agent rarely meets an enemy, so the combat/freeze sub-policies get far less reward signal and far less practice. This is your point, and it's real.
  3. Reward balance shifts. Your per-tile goal_step shaping telescopes to roughly initial_distance × 0.1, which ~doubles on 20×20, while terminal rewards (key, win) stay fixed. So navigation shaping increasingly dominates the terminal objectives.
  4. The network got 4× heavier exactly where it's weakest. SmallGridCNN is stride-1 / same-padding throughout, so it never downsamples — the flatten feeds 64 × grid² into the linear layer: 6,400 → 25,600 inputs (~3.3M params in that one layer). 4× the parameters, a harder credit-assignment problem, and
  presumably the same training budget → undertrained.
  5. Spawn constraints don't scale. key ≥ 2, exit ≥ 4, melee > 4 are tiny on a 20×20 map, so you get a weird mix of "everything spawned in one corner" trivial episodes and genuinely long ones.

  What actually compensates (roughly in order of impact)

  - Make the size-dependent constants relative to grid_size. Scale step_limit, the speed-bonus denominator, and the spawn-distance minimums with the map. This is the cheapest fix and probably recovers a lot on its own.
  - Scale density with area, not count. Keep interactions-per-area constant: ~4× the enemies, walls, and freeze pickups. Your channel design already supports this for free — melee share one channel, and you can spawn multiple warlocks into the warlock channel the same way. This directly fixes #2.
  - Switch to an egocentric (player-centered) observation — the single biggest architectural lever. Feed a fixed-size window (say 11×11) centered on the player instead of the whole board. Benefits: the network input size becomes independent of map size, local combat perception is identical at any scale,
  and it generalizes/transfers across sizes. Keep your distance_map channel for global navigation (it already encodes "which way to the goal" so the crop doesn't lose the objective). This makes 20×20 barely harder than 10×10 for the policy.
  - Curriculum / transfer. Train on 10×10, then fine-tune on 20×20. This only works cleanly if the network head is size-invariant — i.e., egocentric crop, or an AdaptiveAvgPool2d/strided-conv head so the conv weights transfer (right now the flatten→linear layer can't transfer because its input dim
  changes).
  - Just train longer with more envs. A 4× state space genuinely needs more samples; bump total timesteps, parallel envs, and possibly features_dim and the entropy coefficient for exploration.

  My actual recommendation

  If the point of your project is the interesting part — the multi-objective tradeoffs (key vs. guard, freeze-vs-warlock penalty, shoot vs. flee) — then grid size is a distraction, and I'd stay at 10×10 (or a modest 12–14) and spend the effort on behavioral richness and clean evaluation instead. The 20×20
  result mostly tests navigation and exploration under a fixed compute budget, which isn't where your design is novel. Also note the practical cost: rejection-sampling valid maps, the per-step BFS, and longer episodes mean wall-clock training grows more than 4×.

  If you do want to keep 20×20 — e.g., to show generalization across map sizes, which would be a genuinely nice result for the writeup — then commit to it properly: relative constants + scaled density + egocentric observation + curriculum. That combination is what makes large grids work, and the
  egocentric+curriculum angle gives you a real story ("trained on 10×10, generalizes to 20×20") rather than just a worse number.

● That's a sharp question, and your intuition is largely right — let me validate it precisely, because the answer is "yes, for what you actually want."

  Will a big level-clear reward make it go to the exit? Yes.

  The agent's decision at a cleared level is essentially:

  go to exit:  R_clear  +  γ^k · E[value of next level]
  don't:       ≈ 0  (idle → truncate)

  Right now E[value of next level] is negative (it dies there), and R_clear = 1.0 isn't big enough to outweigh that discounted loss, so idling wins. If you make R_clear large enough to dominate the discounted next-level loss, finishing flips to positive EV and the agent will reliably clear the board, grab
  the key, and run to the exit to collect it. With γ=0.99 and death ~20–40 steps into the next level, that discounted loss is only about −0.5 to −0.7, so a clear reward of 3.0 crushes it. Your instinct is correct.
